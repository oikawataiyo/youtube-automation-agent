World War II. Allied bombers are getting shredded over Europe and the military has a problem. Planes are heavy. Fuel is precious. You can't armor everything — extra metal means less range, less payload, more weight. So they do what feels obvious. They study the bombers that come back. They map every bullet hole on every returning plane. The pattern is clear. Holes everywhere on the wings, the tail, the fuselage. Almost nothing on the engines or the cockpit. So the conclusion is easy. Armor the wings. Armor the tail. That's where the bullets are landing. Then, working at Columbia University's Statistical Research Group in a series of memoranda in 1943, a mathematician named Abraham Wald looks at the exact same data and says something that changes how the entire 20th century thinks about evidence. He says — you're looking at the wrong planes. The bullet holes you're mapping, all those red dots on the wings and the tail — those are the holes a bomber can survive. The clean areas, the engines and the cockpit — those aren't safe spots. Those are the places where, when a bullet lands there, the plane doesn't come home to be measured at all. The armor needs to go where there are no holes. Wald was right. The military reinforced the engines and the cockpit. Thousands of pilots came home who otherwise would not have. And in the process, Wald named — quietly, by accident, in a stack of internal memos no one outside the military read for decades — the most important error in your entire decision-making toolkit. Survivorship bias. The systematic mistake of drawing conclusions from cases that survived a hidden selection process, while invisibly ignoring the ones that didn't. The founders you read about. The influencers on your feed. The friend who quit their job and made it work. Those are the planes that came back. Everything you're missing is in the silence. And here's the part that should disturb you: this isn't a small effect. It's the engine driving almost every visible success story you've ever absorbed. In 1973, a paper appeared in Cognitive Psychology — Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman, who would later define an entire field. The paper was called Availability: A Heuristic for Judging Frequency and Probability, and it described a shortcut your brain runs on every day. You judge how likely or important something is by how easily examples come to mind. Sounds harmless. It is not. Because examples don't come to mind based on truth. They come to mind based on visibility. Successful founders write books. Failed founders update their résumés. Successful actors give interviews. Failed actors get other jobs. Successful funds advertise their returns. Failed funds quietly close — and disappear from the database entirely. A 1992 study by Brown, Goetzmann, Ibbotson and Ross in the Review of Financial Studies found that scrubbing dead funds from the historical record inflated reported returns by roughly 0.2 to 0.8 percent every year. Doesn't sound like much. Over thirty years of compounding, it's the difference between a comfortable retirement and a delayed one. And later analyses have pushed the estimate higher. The investor who looks at past performance isn't seeing a fair sample. They're seeing the bombers that came back, lined up in rows, all bullet holes and reassurance. That's layer one. We're roughly a third of the way in, and if you walked out of here knowing only that, you'd already be ahead of most of the people in your industry. But layer one is the cheap version.
