## SECTION OUTLINES (5 sections, ~320 words each)

### Section 1 — "The Myth That Won't Die"
- The widely cited "human attention span fell to 8 seconds, less than a goldfish" claim, traced back to a 2015 Microsoft Canada Consumer Insights brief — and the fact that no peer-reviewed primary source supports it (a fact-check tradition has documented this since BBC and Policy Review reporting).
- Mark, G. (2023) *Attention Span* — actual measured average focus on a single screen has dropped from ~2.5 minutes in 2004 to ~47 seconds in the early 2020s.
- The distinction that matters: it isn't that we *cannot* sustain attention — it's that we *don't*. The capacity is intact. The incentive isn't.
- Close on: a story about a damaged organ is comforting. The truth is, the organ is fine — the environment changed.

### Section 2 — "Dopamine Is Not Pleasure"
- Schultz, Dayan & Montague (1997, *Science*) — dopamine neurons fire to *prediction error*, not reward itself. The brain pays you for surprise, not for the thing.
- Berridge & Robinson (1998, *Brain Research Reviews*) — the wanting / liking distinction; dopamine drives wanting, not satisfaction.
- Why this matters: a system that fires on unpredictable reward is more reinforcing than one that fires on certain reward. (Cite Skinner's variable-ratio schedules as the original lab finding.)
- Close on: every app on your phone is, in neurochemical terms, a slot machine with prettier UI.

### Section 3 — "The Casino In Your Pocket"
- Fogg, B. J. (2003) *Persuasive Technology* — the academic ancestor of the modern engagement industry, originating in Stanford's Persuasive Tech Lab.
- Eyal, N. (2014) *Hooked* — the explicit framework: trigger → action → variable reward → investment.
- Pull-to-refresh as a slot lever (cite Tristan Harris commentary tradition and the broader Center for Humane Technology critique).
- Real time-spent data: average global daily phone use of ~3-4 hours; Common Sense Media (2021) on teen daily screen time crossing 8 hours.
- Close on: the goldfish didn't change. The aquarium became a casino.

### Section 4 — "What Happens To A Brain That Lives Here"
- Ophir, Nass & Wagner (2009, *PNAS*) — heavy media multitaskers performed worse on attention-switching and filtering tasks, contradicting the assumption that multitasking trains itself.
- Wilmer, Sherman & Chein (2017, *Frontiers in Psychology*) — smartphone use correlates with reduced sustained attention and working memory in laboratory measures.
- Hari, J. (2022) *Stolen Focus* — synthesizes the literature with sleep disruption, environment fragmentation, and intentional engineering as compounding factors.
- The deepest cost isn't IQ. It's the slow erosion of long-form thought — the kind that built every meaningful project a viewer ever cared about.
- Close on: the brain doesn't ask whether the next dopamine bid is good for you.

### Section 5 — "Reclaiming The Circuit"
- The "dopamine fasting" myth (briefly debunked — Sepah's 2019 protocol was misinterpreted into pop bro-science; the literature shows you can't drain dopamine by avoiding fun).
- Friction design — Newport, C. (2016) *Deep Work*. Make distractions costly; make focus the default.
- Single-tabbing, monotasking, and the *minimum effective ritual* — the small structural choices that beat willpower.
- Attention restoration via low-stimulation environments — Kaplan (1995, *Journal of Environmental Psychology*) — Attention Restoration Theory.
- Identity move — "I am the kind of person whose attention is expensive" — short, behavioral, repeatable.
- Close on: you don't win this war by being stronger. You win by becoming a worse customer.
