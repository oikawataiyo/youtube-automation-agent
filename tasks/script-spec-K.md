## SECTION OUTLINES (5 sections, ~320 words each)

### Section 1 — "The Blueprint You Did Not Choose"
- Bowlby (1969, *Attachment and Loss, Vol. 1*) — the attachment behavioral system; infants are built to seek a caregiver under threat.
- Ainsworth et al. (1978, *Patterns of Attachment*) — the Strange Situation; avoidant infants who, when distressed, do *not* seek the caregiver and instead self-soothe and look away — yet their heart rate says they are anything but calm.
- Close on: the avoidant child learned that needing someone did not pay, so it stopped showing the need — not stopped having it.

### Section 2 — "From the Crib to the Bedroom"
- Hazan & Shaver (1987, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*) — adult romantic love runs on the same attachment system; the patterns carry forward.
- Bartholomew & Horowitz (1991, *JPSP*) — a four-category model; "dismissing-avoidant" (a positive self-model, a negative model of others, "I don't need anyone") versus "fearful-avoidant" (wants closeness, terrified of it).
- Fraley & Shaver (2000, *Review of General Psychology*) — attachment as continuous dimensions of anxiety and avoidance, not boxes.
- Close on: the strategy that protected a four-year-old is now sabotaging a thirty-year-old's relationships.

### Section 3 — "Deactivating Strategies"
- Mikulincer & Shaver (2007, *Attachment in Adulthood*) — avoidant people use *deactivating* strategies: suppress attachment needs, inflate self-reliance, push away cues of dependence.
- Cassidy & Kobak (1988) — avoidance as a defensive process that keeps the attachment system switched off to avoid the pain of expected rejection.
- Fraley, Davis & Shaver (1998) — dismissing-avoidance reorganizes attention and memory to keep intimacy at arm's length.
- Close on: pulling away is not the absence of feeling — it is a tool for managing feeling that is too big to hold.

### Section 4 — "What This Is Quietly Costing"
- Simpson, Rholes & Nelligan (1992, *JPSP*) — under stress, avoidant partners seek less support and give less; the moment connection is most needed, the strategy fires hardest.
- Gross & Levenson (1997, *Journal of Abnormal Psychology*) — habitually hiding emotion has a physiological cost and does not actually reduce the inner experience, only the sharing of it.
- The partner reads withdrawal as rejection; the avoidant reads closeness as engulfment; both are reacting to a forecast, not the person.
- Close on: the wall that kept the child safe is now keeping the adult alone.

### Section 5 — "How the Pattern Changes"
- Earned security. Roisman et al. (2002, *Child Development*) — people with rough early attachment can develop "earned-secure" status; the past is a starting point, not a sentence.
- Felt security and a steady partner. Mikulincer & Shaver (2007) — repeated experiences of a responsive other can revise the internal model.
- Naming the deactivation in real time — recognizing the urge to flee as the strategy, not the truth.
- Structured repair. Johnson (2008, *Hold Me Tight*) — Emotionally Focused Therapy rebuilds the bond by making the withdrawal itself the thing the couple talks about.
- Close on: you do not delete the blueprint; you let a new experience overwrite it, slowly.

## OUTRO BEATS
- Avoidant attachment is not coldness — it is love that learned to expect the door to close, so it leaves first.
- The strategy was smart once; it is just running in the wrong decade.
- Sign-off line.
