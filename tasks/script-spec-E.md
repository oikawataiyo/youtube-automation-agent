## SECTION OUTLINES (5 sections, ~320 words each)

### Section 1 — "The 2-to-1 Asymmetry"
- **Kahneman & Tversky (1979) — Prospect theory: an analysis of decision under risk (*Econometrica*)** — the foundational paper. The value function bends sharply at zero; losses register roughly 2x as steeply as equivalent gains.
- **Tversky & Kahneman (1991) — Loss aversion in riskless choice: a reference-dependent model (*Quarterly Journal of Economics*)** — extends the principle beyond gambling to ordinary preference.
- Concrete example: most subjects demand around $200 in possible gain to risk a $100 loss in a coin-flip framing.
- Close on: this is not character. It is architecture.

### Section 2 — "Why It Is Built In"
- Negativity bias as the broader system. **Rozin & Royzman (2001) — Negativity bias, negativity dominance, and contagion (*Personality and Social Psychology Review*)** — across affect, cognition, and learning, negative outcomes weigh more.
- **Baumeister, Bratslavsky, Finkenauer & Vohs (2001) — Bad is stronger than good (*Review of General Psychology*)** — landmark synthesis. The asymmetry is documented across relationships, memory, parenting, learning.
- Evolutionary frame: false alarms about danger are cheap, missed alarms are sometimes fatal. Cite **Haselton & Buss (2000) — Error management theory (*Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*)** for the adaptive logic of asymmetric error tolerance.
- Close on: the system is not broken. It is calibrated for a world where you only had to be wrong about a predator once.

### Section 3 — "What It Distorts In Modern Life"
- Financial behavior — **Benartzi & Thaler (1995) — Myopic loss aversion and the equity premium puzzle (*Quarterly Journal of Economics*)** — investors check portfolios too often, feel each dip twice as hard, and underweight equities for decades.
- Memory bias — **Baumeister et al. (2001)** documents that critical feedback is recalled longer and in more detail than equivalent praise.
- Status quo bias and endowment effect — **Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler (1990) — Experimental tests of the endowment effect and the Coase theorem (*Journal of Political Economy*)** — once you own something, parting with it feels like a 2x larger loss than acquiring it felt like a gain.
- Close on: the same circuit that protected your ancestor from a 1-in-1000 predator is now talking you out of a 50-50 career move.

### Section 4 — "What Makes It Worse Online"
- Social comparison amplification — **Vogel, Rose, Roberts & Eckles (2014) — Social comparison, social media, and self-esteem (*Psychology of Popular Media Culture*)** — feeds curate other people's gains and your own losses become the salient reference point.
- Algorithmic outrage — **Brady, Wills, Jost, Tucker & Van Bavel (2017) — Emotion shapes the diffusion of moralized content in social networks (*PNAS*)** — negative-emotion content travels further, training attention onto threat signal.
- Loss-framed political and advertising messaging — **Tversky & Kahneman (1981) — The framing of decisions and the psychology of choice (*Science*)** — the same option flipped from "save 200 lives" to "let 400 die" produces opposite choice patterns.
- Close on: the bias is being industrially monetized, and the meter is your attention.

### Section 5 — "How To Trade On It Instead Of Be Eaten By It"
- **Thaler & Sunstein (2008) — *Nudge: Improving Decisions About Health, Wealth, and Happiness* (Yale)** — loss-aversion-aware defaults (organ donation opt-out, 401k auto-enrollment) save lives and accounts.
- Personal use — set up the structure so the avoided outcome is the bad one, not the action. Cite **Gneezy, Meier & Rey-Biel (2011) — When and why incentives (don't) work to modify behavior (*Journal of Economic Perspectives*)** on incentive design.
- Memory hygiene — **Roy F. Baumeister (2001)** implication: deliberately re-encode wins (write them down) because the system will not do this for you.
- Identity reframe — being averse to loss is not cowardice. It is your default risk model. You override it the way pilots override instinct in instrument flight: with checklists, not willpower.
- Close on: you cannot delete the 2-to-1. You can stop letting it pick your life.
