## SECTION OUTLINES (5 sections, ~320 words each)

### Section 1 — "The Alarm That Goes Off Before the Fire"
- The amygdala as a fast threat-detector that triggers before the cortex finishes interpreting. LeDoux (1996, *The Emotional Brain*) — the "low road" that arms the body before you consciously know why.
- LeDoux & Pine (2016, *American Journal of Psychiatry*) — a two-system framework: defensive survival circuits (body) are distinct from the conscious *feeling* of fear/anxiety. The alarm and the story about the alarm are separate machines.
- Close on: by the time you "feel" anxious, the forecast has already moved your body.

### Section 2 — "Intolerance of the Unknown"
- Grupe & Nitschke (2013, *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*) — uncertainty and anticipation are the engine of anxiety; the anxious brain over-estimates threat probability and cost, and under-tolerates not knowing.
- Dugas et al. (1998, *Behaviour Research and Therapy*) — intolerance of uncertainty as a core driver of generalized anxiety.
- Carleton (2016, *Journal of Anxiety Disorders*) — "fear of the unknown" proposed as the one fundamental fear beneath the others.
- Close on: anxiety is not fear of a thing; it is the refusal to sit inside a question.

### Section 3 — "The Body Writes the Prediction"
- Barrett & Simmons (2015, *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*) — the brain runs interoceptive predictions, guessing the body's next state before signals arrive. Anxiety is a prediction of a bad bodily state that then helps produce it.
- Paulus & Stein (2006, *Biological Psychiatry*) — an insular/interoceptive view: anxious brains amplify the predicted-vs-actual gap and treat normal body noise as evidence of threat.
- Clark (2013, *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*) — predictive processing: perception is controlled hallucination; anxiety is a forecast the body then confirms.
- Close on: the racing heart is not proof of danger — it is the forecast made flesh.

### Section 4 — "The Loop That Keeps the Forecast Alive"
- Avoidance as the quiet villain. Borkovec et al. (2004) — worry itself is a cognitive avoidance strategy that blunts the feared image and prevents the brain from learning it was safe.
- Salkovskis (1991, *Behavioural Psychotherapy*) — "safety behaviours" (the seat by the door, the rehearsed exit) prevent disconfirmation, so the threat forecast is never corrected.
- The relief of avoidance is a teacher: every escape tells the brain the danger was real.
- Close on: anxiety survives because you keep proving it right by running.

### Section 5 — "How to Recalibrate the Forecast"
- Inhibitory learning over fear-extinction-as-erasure. Craske et al. (2014, *Behaviour Research and Therapy*) — exposure works by building a new, stronger "it's safe" prediction alongside the old one, especially under expectancy violation.
- Acceptance instead of suppression. Hayes, Strosahl & Wilson (1999) — Acceptance and Commitment Therapy: stop fighting the forecast, act in spite of it.
- Tolerating uncertainty as a trainable skill — small, deliberate doses of not-knowing.
- Interoceptive re-labeling — naming the racing heart as a prediction, not a verdict.
- Close on: you do not delete the alarm; you teach it a better estimate.

## OUTRO BEATS
- The anxious brain is not broken — it is a forecaster that loves you too aggressively and trusts the worst data.
- You cannot stop it from predicting; you can change what it learns from.
- Sign-off line.
