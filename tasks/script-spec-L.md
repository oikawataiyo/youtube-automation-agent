## SECTION OUTLINES (5 sections, ~320 words each)

### Section 1 — "It Was Never About Time"
- Steel (2007, *Psychological Bulletin*) — the definitive meta-analysis: procrastination correlates with task aversiveness, impulsiveness, and low self-efficacy, not with poor scheduling or a packed calendar.
- The reframe: procrastination is a self-regulatory failure, not a planning failure. Better calendars do not fix it because the calendar was never the problem.
- Close on: you do not have a time problem; you have a feeling you are trying not to have.

### Section 2 — "The Mood-Repair Trap"
- Sirois & Pychyl (2013, *Social and Personality Psychology Compass*) — procrastination is the prioritization of short-term mood over long-term goals: "giving in to feel good." The task triggers an aversive emotion; delay relieves it now.
- Tice, Bratslavsky & Baumeister (2001, *JPSP*) — when people are upset, regulating the bad mood takes precedence over impulse control; soothing now beats the future self.
- Close on: every delay is a tiny, effective dose of relief — which is exactly why it is so hard to stop.

### Section 3 — "The Brain That Discounts the Future"
- Ainslie (1975, *Psychological Bulletin*) — hyperbolic discounting: rewards are valued far less the further away they sit, so the small immediate relief out-bids the large delayed payoff.
- McClure, Laibson, Loewenstein & Cohen (2004, *Science*) — separate neural systems value immediate versus delayed rewards; limbic circuits light up for "now," prefrontal for "later," and "now" often wins the auction.
- Close on: your future self is a stranger your brain is happy to rob.

### Section 4 — "The Bill Comes Due"
- Tice & Baumeister (1997, *Psychological Science*) — the longitudinal study: procrastinators feel less stress and better early in the term, then end with worse health, higher stress, and lower performance. The relief is real and the cost is deferred.
- Gustavson et al. (2014, *Psychological Science*) — procrastination and impulsivity share a genetic basis tied to failures of goal-management; it is an ancient by-product of a present-tuned brain, not a moral defect.
- Close on: procrastination is a high-interest loan against a self you have not met yet.

### Section 5 — "How to Stop Negotiating With the Feeling"
- Self-compassion over self-flagellation. Sirois (2014, *Self and Identity*) — being harsh worsens the cycle; self-forgiveness reduces future procrastination by lowering the aversive load.
- Implementation intentions. Gollwitzer (1999, *American Psychologist*) — "if X, then I will do Y" pre-decides the action so the in-the-moment feeling never gets a vote.
- Shrink the aversiveness — make the first step absurdly small so the dread has nothing to grab.
- Name the emotion at the door instead of letting it drive you to the dishes.
- Close on: you do not beat procrastination by trying harder; you beat it by changing what the task feels like to start.

## OUTRO BEATS
- Procrastination is your brain being a loving, short-sighted parent: protecting you from a feeling at the future's expense.
- The task was never the enemy — the emotion under it was, and that you can actually work with.
