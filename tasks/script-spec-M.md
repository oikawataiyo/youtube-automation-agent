## SECTION OUTLINES (5 sections, ~320 words each)

### Section 1 — "The Lawyer in Your Head"
- Wason (1960, *Quarterly Journal of Experimental Psychology*) — the 2-4-6 task: people test rules by seeking confirmations, almost never trying to disprove their own hypothesis.
- Nickerson (1998, *Review of General Psychology*) — confirmation bias as a ubiquitous, cross-domain tendency to seek, weight, and remember evidence that fits what we already believe.
- Close on: you are not a scientist gathering data; you are a lawyer building a case for a client you cannot fire — yourself.

### Section 2 — "Reasoning Was Built to Argue"
- Mercier & Sperber (2011, *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*) — the argumentative theory: human reasoning evolved not to reach accurate beliefs alone, but to produce and evaluate arguments in social debate. Confirmation bias is a feature of a tool built for persuasion.
- Seen this way, the bias is not a bug in the truth-machine; the truth-machine was always a persuasion-machine.
- Close on: your mind is excellent at reasoning — it is just optimized for winning, not for being right.

### Section 3 — "Motivated Reasoning"
- Kunda (1990, *Psychological Bulletin*) — the case for motivated reasoning: what we *want* to be true shapes which evidence we scrutinize and which we wave through.
- Westen et al. (2006, *Journal of Cognitive Neuroscience*) — when partisans process threatening information about their side, the brain recruits emotion-regulation circuits, not cold reasoning circuits, and is rewarded for reaching the comfortable conclusion.
- Close on: you do not believe what the evidence says; you believe what protects how you already feel.

### Section 4 — "When Being Smarter Makes It Worse"
- Taber & Lodge (2006, *American Journal of Political Science*) — "motivated skepticism": people accept agreeable evidence at face value but mount a furious counter-argument against anything that threatens their view.
- Kahan et al. (2017, *Behavioural Public Policy*) — "motivated numeracy": on politically charged data, the *most* numerate people become *more* polarized, using their skill to rationalize, not to correct.
- Close on: intelligence is not a defense against this — it is a better weapon for the side you already picked.

### Section 5 — "How to Actually Update"
- Consider the opposite. Lord, Lepper & Preston (1984, *JPSP*) — deliberately asking "what would make me wrong?" measurably reduces biased evaluation.
- Outsource the disconfirmation — Mercier & Sperber (2011): reasoning works best in groups that disagree, because someone else will attack your weak argument for you.
- Track your predictions. Tetlock & Gardner (2015, *Superforecasting*) — accuracy comes from scoring your forecasts and updating in small increments, not from conviction.
- Hold identity loosely — Leary et al. (2017, *Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin*) on intellectual humility: separating "I was wrong" from "I am worthless" is what makes updating survivable.
- Close on: you cannot delete the lawyer; you can force it to argue both sides.

## OUTRO BEATS
- Confirmation bias is not a sign you are stupid — it is a sign your brain is doing exactly what it was built to do.
- The fix is never "be more rational"; it is to build systems that make you confront the case against you.
- Sign-off line.
